1. Introduction

Hong Kong is situated on the southeastern coast of China and is spread out over some 1,100 square kilometers, including 260 outlying islands. The main areas are Hong Kong Island, Kowloon Peninsula, and the New Territories. Hong Kong Island lies just south of Kowloon, separated by Victoria Harbor; the New Territories is north of Kowloon, and borders mainland China.

Hong Kong was a collection of fishing villages when claimed as a Crown Colony by Britain in 1841. It was first described as a "barren rock," much to the amusement of today's property barons. As the original British presence grew from Hong Kong Island to include Kowloon Peninsula and the New Territories, it thrived as a trading port and gateway to China.

Though the founding of the Republic of China, the Sino-Japanese War, the Second World War, and the revolution of 1949 all rocked the territory, they also provided new vigor, in the form of refugees who would eventually take their place in the territory's teeming workplace.

In the l950s, Hong Kong began in earnest a new career as a manufacturing and industrial center. Textile, electronics, watches, and many other low-priced goods stamped "Made in Hong Kong" flowed from the territory in ever-increasing amounts.

Today the financial service industries have taken over from manufacturing as Hong Kong's main enterprise; it's the world's fifth-largest banking and financial center. This small territory was also the first developing economy to enter the world's top 10 economies. And while much of the manufacturing is now done across the border and beyond, Hong Kong is still one of the world's largest exporters.

1 July 1997 marked the historic date of Hong Kong's reversion to Chinese sovereignty. With the resumption of the exercise of sovereignty over Hong Kong, China has implemented the principle of "one country, two systems" and fulfilled the promises of "high degree of autonomy" and "Hong Kong people administering Hong Kong". With great vision, China's leaders have formulated the unique "one country, two systems" concept and honored a solemn commitment to set a clear example for the future reunification of our entire nation. We can advance into the 21st Century with conviction, prudence and determination, and with the goal of achieving even greater success.

2. IT Research in Hong Kong

Over the past two years, the HKSAR Government has been taking initiatives to promote its IT vision for Hong Kong. It has allocated US$323 million for IT provisions in schools. Hong Kong is also in the middle of an Electric Service Delivery (EDS) project that costs around US$22 million. This will provide 24-hour public services via the Internet and the infrastructure later will be opened to private firms for electronic commerce.

The establishment of the Applied Science and Technology Research Institute enhances the University-industry collaboration and mid-stream research.

The HKSAR Government will join hands with the Pacific Century Group, one of the leaders in developing interactive information services, in developing a world-class Cyberport, providing the first class infrastructure for the formation of a strategic cluster of top international IT companies in Hong Kong.

3. Hong Kong an International City

Hong Kong is one of the world's most modern and successful cities. Many factors have contributed towards Hong Kong's international reputation as both a leading manufacturing complex and a major commercial center in Asia. These include an economic policy of free enterprise and free trade, an industrious workforce, a sophisticated commercial infrastructure, one of the finest ports in the world, a strategically located airport and excellent communication network worldwide.

Commerce, Trade & Industry

Hong Kong, the gateway to Asia (China in particular), has created numerous business opportunities and attracted overseas investments. Over 5,312 overseas corporations from 76 countries are registered in Hong Kong.

Hong Kong is a free port. There is no import or export control other than those exercised for health and security reasons. Formality is kept to a minimum. Convention and exhibition organizers and participants can bring in their materials with little customs restrictions.

  • In l998, domestic exports and re-exports amounted to a total over US$170 billion.
  • Enjoys a worldwide reputation as a producer and exporter of manufactured consumer goods.
  • The leading exporter of textiles, clothing, watches and clocks.
  • Hong Kong was the world's 8 largest trading economy in 1998.

Finance

Hong Kong is a major international financial center. Around 474 foreign-owned licensed banks, restricted license banks and deposit-taking companies (including representative offices) from over 40 countries operate in Hong Kong, including 79 of the world's top 100 banks in terms of capital.

  • Largest foreign direct investor in the region, and fourth in the world.
  • An important center for the financing and organizing of capital-intensive regional infrastructure projects such as ports, highways, bridges and power plants, with a large portion of the overall outward investment from Hong Kong.
  • Hong Kong is one of the world's leading financial center - one of the largest gold bullion markets in the world, 7th largest in terms of the volume of external banking transactions; and 11th largest in terms of stock market capitalization in the world and 2nd in Asia.

Currency and Tax Policy

Since the mid-eighties, the Hong Kong Dollar has been pegged to the US Dollar at HK$7.80 to US$1.00. The stability of the Hong Kong Dollar makes budgeting easier for both conference organizers as well as delegates.

Hong Kong's tax system is one of the simplest in the world with its tax rates among the lowest in the world. No tax is levied on profits arising from the sale of capital assets or on offshore trading profits.

Accessibility/Air Transportation Facilities

Located at the heart of Asia, Hong Kong is highly accessible. There are 65 airlines serving over 100 destinations worldwide, with more than 1,500 scheduled passenger and cargo flights each week.

  • 33% of the world's population is within 5 flying hours
  • 52% of the world's population is within 8 flying hours

A new International Airport at Chek Lap Kok off Lantau Island commenced operation on 6 July 1998. It has a capacity of handling 35 million passengers a year.

Visitors can reach the airport by various means of transportation: Airport Express train, Mass Transit Railway (MTR), Airbus, regular city bus, ferry or taxi. The quickest connection from downtown Central district is the Airport Express train, operated by the MTR. It departs every 10 minutes and travels to and from Central in just 23 minutes. Visitors can then travel via MTR to districts throughout Kowloon and Hong Kong Island. The Airbus, which stops at most major hotels, is a good alternative. Taxis can take visitors from the airport to virtually anywhere in Hong Kong.

Public Transport

Hong Kong, geographically compact with excellent public transportation.

  • Taxis - Fares are comparatively low. The basic flag fall rate is HK$15 (US$1.92) for the first 2 kilometers in Kowloon and on Hong Kong Island, and the meter adds on HK$1.40 (US$0.18) every 200 meters.
  • Buses - Double-deckers and coaches have their final destinations marked in English and Chinese on the front top panel. Fares are low (ranging from HK$1.20 (US$0.15) to HK$45 (US$5.8))
  • Ferries - The Star Ferry, Hong Kong's most scenic (and inexpensive) mode of travel across Victoria Harbor, celebrated 100 years of service in 1998. Other ferry services are available to the New Territories, outlying islands as well as mainland China and Macau.
  • Minibuses - Minibuses are small passenger vans that differ by their color. Fares for both are bit higher than buses, but usually in the HK$1.50 (US$0.19) to HK$20 (US$2.57) range.
  • Trains - The Mass Transit Railway (MTR) is fast, clean, efficient and inexpensive. The Kowloon Canton Railway (KCR) is a commuter railway that runs from Hung Hom in Kowloon to the boundary with mainland China. The Light Rail (LR) connects the New Territories towns of Tuen Mun and Yuen Long.
  • Peak Tram - A quick way to Hong Kong's highest point. This cable railway has been operating since 1888, and climbs 373 meters to Victoria Peak in eight minutes.
  • Hongkong Tramways - Running along the harbor side of Hong Kong Island, double-decker trams rock, rattle and roll along the same tracks they've traveled since 1904, offering one of the best views in town. Flat fare only at HK$2 ~ (US$0.25).
  • Car Hire - Chauffeur-driven cars are widely available.

Communications

  • Hong Kong is the first major city in the world to have a fully digitalized telephone network. The telephone density is the highest in Asia and one of the highest in the world.
  • International Direct Dialing covers over 230 overseas destinations and more than 1,100 cities in China, and is available in all guestrooms of major hotels. About 241 minutes of outgoing international phone call (excluding facsimile and data) per head of population in 1997 - much higher than both the US and Japan.
  • Mobile phone and radio paging services subscribers in Hong Kong are among the highest in the world.
  • The second highest rate of business fax usage in the world.
  • The postal service in Hong Kong is one of the best in the world in terms of delivery time, reliability and charges. 98% of local ordinary letters achieve next day delivery.

Entry Regulations

Hong Kong has a liberal visa policy whereby nationals of more than 170 countries can visit Hong Kong without a visa for periods of seven days to six months. Please refer to http://www.info.gov.hk/ for more details.

Climate

Hong Kong has a subtropical climate with district seasonal changes. The most popular time to visit is from the middle of September to the end of February. In autumn (late September - early December), temperature and humidity drop. Clear and sunny days with temperatures range from 18¢X - 28¢XC (64¢X - 82¢XF). Humidity averages 72 percent.

Safety

Hong Kong is a remarkably safe city by day or night. The overall crime rate of Hong Kong at roughly the same level as Singapore, and lower than Tokyo, London and other metropolitan cities in the United States and Canada. The policemen to population ratio is one of the highest in the world.

Population and language

More than 6.5 million people live in Hong Kong, making some districts among the world's most densely populated areas. Yet Hong Kong has one of the world's highest ratios of conserved parkland with some 40% designated as protected Country Park.

Almost 96% of the population is Chinese. Chinese and English are the official languages. Cantonese is the most widely spoken Chinese dialect, though use of Mandarin (Putonghua) is growing. All major signage is in English or is bilingual. There will be no problems in communicating with people in English in most hotels, restaurants and even some taxis.

Hong Kong Island

1. Wanchai District (Total 4,157 rooms)
One of the oldest residential and a newly developing commercial district. Its northern shore has changed dramatically in the past few years. Huge reclamation schemes have created new land from the harbor for modern flyovers, multistory office buildings and hotels, including the Central Plaza, the tallest concrete-framed building in Asia and the fourth-tallest building in the world.

Amidst the varied delights of these modern developments, many traditional and historic attractions remain. And in the evenings, the glow of neon lights still lends the area a uniquely nostalgic atmosphere.

2. Causeway Bay (Total 2,482 rooms)
Lying to the east of Wanchai, Causeway Bay area is a popular local shopping and entertainment district. A cluster of Japanese department stores, street markets open until late night, and restaurants make it busy both day and night.

3. Central District (Total 1,274 rooms)
The business and financial hub of the territory, Central is a glittering showcase of mirrored towers, multi-million dollar high-rises, designer shops and luxury hotels. Behind this modern and rather western facade lies charming reminders of Hong Kong's colonial past, unexpected pockets of greenery and a soul that is unequivocally Chinese.

4. Queensway (Total 1,680 rooms)
Originally the Victoria Barracks which housed the army for over a century, it is now a newly developed area right on the subway inter-junction between Hong Kong Island and Kowloon Peninsula. The modern shopping mall complex is connected to the three hotels and the subway, boasting numerous shops, restaurants and entertainment options.

Kowloon Peninsula

1. Tsimshatsui (Total 13,502 rooms)
The Kowloon Peninsula's old town which has now developed into Hong Kong's most modern and busiest district, with a cluster of attractions and activities to suit everybody's interest: the Harbor City - Asia's largest shopping centre; Hong Kong Cultural Centre - the largest venue for the performing arts in the territory; the Hong Kong Space Museum, Hong Kong Arts Museum..., just to name a few.

2. Yaumatei/Mongkok (Total 3,255 rooms)
Yaumatei is one of the oldest district of Kowloon, famous for its open-air markets and temples. Mongkok is an exhilaratingly crowded area with open-air markets. These two districts are opening up rapidly for hotel facility development.
